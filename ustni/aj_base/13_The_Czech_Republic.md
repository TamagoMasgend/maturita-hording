# 13 - The Czech Republic

## 1. Geographical Location
The Czech Republic is a landlocked country situated right in the middle of Europe. It shares borders with Poland to the northeast, Slovakia to the east, Austria to the south, and Germany to the west and northwest.

## 2. Area and Population
It covers an area of 78,864 square kilometres. The total population is approximately 10.5 million inhabitants.

## 3. Mountains and Geography
The geography is very varied. The country is surrounded by extensive mountain ranges: 
- **The Šumava range** and the **Czech Forest** in the southwest.
- **The Ore Mountains** (Krušné hory) in the northwest.
- **The Jizera Mountains** (Jizerské hory) and **the Giant Mountains** (Krkonoše) in the north. (Sněžka, 1,603 m, is the highest peak).
- **The Eagle Mountains** (Orlické hory) in the northeast.
- The boundary between Bohemia and Moravia is formed by the **Bohemian-Moravian Highland** (Českomoravská vrchovina).
- In Moravia, there are wooded mountain ranges of the **Jeseníky** and **Beskydy**.
*(Parts: Lowlands are situated mostly around the Elbe river - Polabská nížina, and in southern Moravia).*

## 4. Rivers and Lakes
- The most important rivers are the **Vltava** (which springs from Šumava) and the **Elbe** (Labe), which flows in the north and continues to Germany and the North Sea.
- In Moravia, the main river is the **Morava**, which flows south to the Danube.
- Natural lakes are rare (e.g., Černé a Čertovo jezero in Šumava), but the country has a rich tradition of artificial fishponds (e.g., Rožmberk in South Bohemia) and large water reservoirs (e.g., Lipno).

## 5. Mineral Resources
The Czech Republic does not possess many raw materials compared to huge countries, but it has:
- **Black coal** (hard coal) located mostly around Ostrava and Karviná.
- **Brown coal** mined in the open-cast mines in North Bohemia.
- **Uranium** and significant recently discovered deposits of **Lithium** in the Krušné hory area.

## 6. Industry
The Czech economy is highly industrialized and export-oriented, with one of the lowest unemployment rates in the EU.
- **Automotive:** The backbone of the economy. **Škoda Auto** (Mladá Boleslav) is the largest employer and exporter. The country also hosts factories for Hyundai (Nošovice) and Toyota (Kolín).
- **Engineering and Metallurgy:** Heavy machinery, tools, and arms production (historically Škoda Works in Plzeň, now Česká zbrojovka in Uherský Brod).
- **Chemical and Pharmaceutical industry:** Production of medicines, plastics, and fertilizers.
- **Food and Beer industry:** The Czech Republic has the highest beer consumption per capita in the world. World-famous brands include Pilsner Urquell and Budweiser Budvar.
- **Traditional crafts:** Bohemian crystal and glassmaking (Moser), porcelain, and textile production.
- **Energy:** The country relies on a mix of nuclear power (Temelín, Dukovany), coal, and growing renewable sources.
- **Agriculture:** Wheat, barley, hops (the Žatec region), potatoes, and sugar beet. South Moravia is known for wine production.
- **Tourism:** Prague alone attracts over 8 million tourists annually, making tourism a significant part of the economy.

## 7. Sámo’s Empire
Sámo’s Empire (7th century) was the first historically documented state unit of Slavic tribes on this territory. It was ruled by a Frankish merchant named Sámo, who successfully defended the territory against the Avars and the Franks.

## 8. Disintegration of the Great Moravian Empire
The Great Moravian Empire existed in the 9th century and brought Christianity to the region (via Cyril and Methodius). Because of the disintegration and eventual collapse of the Great Moravian Empire around 906 CE (caused by internal conflicts and invasions by the nomadic Magyars/Hungarians), the political center shifted to Bohemia.

## 9. The First Prince and the Přemyslid Family
The first historically recorded Czech Prince was Bořivoj I (late 9th century). He was baptized and helped establish Christianity in Bohemia. The Czech tribes were united and headed by the **Přemyslid family**, the first Czech royal dynasty.

## 10. The End of the Přemyslid Dynasty
The Přemyslid dynasty ended "by the sword" in 1306 when the last male heir, King Wenceslaus III, was assassinated in Olomouc. Before their end, these princes and kings had a great impact on Central Europe, expanding their territory significantly through warfare and diplomacy.

## 11. The Throne Went by Dynastic Wedding
After the death of Wenceslaus III, the throne passed to the House of Luxembourg. John of Bohemia gained the throne by dynastic wedding, marrying Wenceslaus III's sister, Elizabeth of Bohemia (Eliška Přemyslovna). This eventually led to the reign of their son, Charles IV.

## 12. Charles IV — The Golden Age
Charles IV (1316–1378) was the most significant Czech king and Holy Roman Emperor. Under his rule, Prague became one of the most beautiful and important cities in Europe. He founded Charles University in 1348 (the first university in Central Europe), built Charles Bridge, St. Vitus Cathedral, and the New Town of Prague. His reign is considered the "Golden Age" of Czech history.

## 13. The Hussite Wars
Jan Hus was a Czech religious reformer who criticized the corruption of the Catholic Church. He was burned at the stake as a heretic in 1415 at the Council of Constance. His death sparked the **Hussite Wars** (1419–1434), a series of religious conflicts in which the Hussites, led by the brilliant military commander **Jan Žižka**, successfully defended Bohemia against several crusades sent by the Pope and the Holy Roman Emperor.

## 14. Habsburg Rule and the Battle of White Mountain
In 1526, the Czech throne passed to the **Habsburg dynasty**, which ruled for nearly 400 years. In 1620, the Czech Protestant nobility was defeated at the **Battle of White Mountain** (Bitva na Bílé Hoře) near Prague. This defeat led to harsh re-Catholicization, loss of Czech political independence, Germanization of public life, and execution of 27 Czech lords on the Old Town Square in 1621.

## 15. The Czech National Revival
In the 19th century, a cultural and linguistic movement called the **Czech National Revival** (České národní obrození) emerged. Czech intellectuals, writers, and scientists worked to restore the Czech language and national identity, which had been suppressed under Habsburg/German dominance. Key figures include Josef Dobrovský, Josef Jungmann, and František Palacký.

## 16. Independent Czechoslovakia (1918)
After the defeat of Austria-Hungary in World War I, **Czechoslovakia** was proclaimed as an independent state on **October 28, 1918**. The first president was **Tomáš Garrigue Masaryk**, considered the "father of the nation." Czechoslovakia became one of the most developed democracies in Europe during the interwar period.

## 17. World War II and the Communist Era
- **1938 — Munich Agreement:** Britain and France allowed Nazi Germany to annex the Czech border regions (Sudetenland) without Czechoslovak consent. In March 1939, Germany occupied the rest and created the Protectorate of Bohemia and Moravia.
- **1945:** Czechoslovakia was liberated by Soviet and American forces. The German-speaking population was expelled from the border regions (the Beneš decrees).
- **1948 — Communist coup:** The Communist Party seized power. Czechoslovakia became a Soviet satellite state with a centrally planned economy, political repression, and censorship.
- **1968 — Prague Spring:** An attempt to reform and liberalize the communist regime, led by Alexander Dubček ("socialism with a human face"), was crushed by a Warsaw Pact invasion in August 1968.

## 18. The Velvet Revolution and the Velvet Divorce
- **1989 — Velvet Revolution:** Peaceful mass protests led to the fall of the communist regime in November 1989. **Václav Havel**, a playwright and dissident, became the first democratic president.
- **Velvet Divorce:** On January 1, 1993, Czechoslovakia peacefully split into two independent countries: the **Czech Republic** and **Slovakia**.

## 19. EU and NATO Membership
- **1999:** The Czech Republic joined **NATO**.
- **2004:** The Czech Republic joined the **European Union**.
- The country has not adopted the Euro and continues to use the Czech Crown (CZK) as its currency.
