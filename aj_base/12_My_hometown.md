# 12 - My hometown (Havraň)

## 1. Basic info

My hometown is Havraň. It is a small village located in the Ústí nad Labem Region (Ústecký kraj), in the Most District (okres Most), approximately 6–7 km southwest of the city of Most. It has a population of around **634 inhabitants**. The village consists of three parts: Havraň, Moravěves, and Saběnice. It is situated in a landscape shaped by the coal-mining industry of the wider Most region.

## 2. Legend

According to a local legend, a hunter was once carrying a wild boar he had caught on his back through the area. He became exhausted from the heavy load and lay down to rest, falling asleep. While he slept, a large raven (in Czech: *havran*) appeared and attacked him, waking him up and saving his life from danger. In gratitude, the man settled on that spot and named the place after the raven — **Havraň**.

## 3. History

- **First written mention:** Havraň was first documented in **1281**, when a man named Chval of Havraň (Qual de Habran) was recorded in historical sources.
- **Medieval period:** Throughout the centuries, the village changed owners multiple times. From the late 15th century, it was administered by the city of Most for some time.
- **Early modern period:** In 1628, Havraň was incorporated into the Kopisty municipal domain, where it remained until serfdom was abolished in 1848.
- **Independent village:** After 1850, Havraň became an independent municipality. The settlements of Moravěves and Saběnice were later attached to it.
- **Post-WWII:** After the Second World War, the original German-speaking population was expelled, and the village was resettled by Czech inhabitants, causing a significant population decline.

## 4. Interesting places in the village

1. **Church of St. Lawrence (Kostel sv. Vavřince):** Originally a Gothic church, rebuilt in the Baroque style in the 18th century. The first records of it as a parish church date back to 1364. It is the main landmark of the village.
2. **Traditional architecture:** The village preserves a mix of urban-style buildings from the turn of the 19th and 20th centuries alongside traditional agricultural homesteads.

## 5. Surroundings and trips nearby

Since Havraň is a small village, the nearby city of **Most** and the surrounding area offer most of the cultural and recreational opportunities:

1. **Hněvín Castle and lookout tower (Most):** The dominant landmark of Most, offering panoramic views of the entire region.
2. **Mostecké jezero (Most Lake):** A large recreational lake created from a former open-pit mine, used for swimming and water sports.
3. **Bořeň:** A famous rocky hill near Bílina, popular among hikers and rock climbers, part of the České středohoří highlands.
4. **Žatec:** A nearby historic town famous as the "City of Hops," with a Hop Museum and the Hop Lighthouse lookout tower.
5. **Jezeří Castle:** A significant castle located under the Ore Mountains (Krušné hory), overlooking the mining landscape.

## 6. Transport

Havraň is connected to the surrounding area mainly by road. Public bus lines connect the village to Most, which serves as the main transport hub of the district. Most has a railway station with connections to Prague, Chomutov, and Ústí nad Labem. The nearest major motorway access is to the D7 (Prague–Chomutov).

## 7. Schools

As a small village, Havraň does not have a secondary school. Children attend elementary school locally or in nearby villages, and for secondary education, students commute to **Most**, which has several grammar schools (gymnázia) and secondary technical schools.

## 8. My opinion

*(Personal input example)* I like living in Havraň because it is a quiet, peaceful village surrounded by nature, yet close enough to Most for shopping, entertainment, and school. It is a safe environment where everybody knows each other. The main disadvantage is that there is not much to do directly in the village, and you depend on bus connections to get to the city.
